WHAT IS HYPNOSIS?

The word hypnosis comes from the Greek work “hypnos”, meaning sleep, resulting from the physical appearance of one sleeping. It is however not sleep.

Hypnosis has multiple definitions. The 2014 revised definition of Hypnosis is:

A state of consciousness involving focused attention and reduced peripheral awareness characterized by an enhanced capacity for response to suggestion.
International Journal of Clinical and Experimental Hypnosis

The definition of “Hypnotherapy”, suggested by the same committee in 2014 is as follows: “The use of hypnosis in the treatment of a medical or psychological disorder or concern.”

As a matter of fact, we all experience altered states of consciousness in our day to day lives, in the form of daydreaming, focused attention, or deep concentration. It is even liken to meditation and prayer. It is important to remember, that all hypnosis is SELF HYPNOSIS – meaning, if you don’t fully agree to the process, if you are not motivated, it can hardly happen.

Hypnosis ultimately taps into the functioning of the unconscious mind. It is the unconscious mind that is like our central processing unit that communicates not only with our conscious mind, but also with our body.

Myths about Hypnosis

There are a number of common myths about hypnosis, resulting from the entertainment industry (i.e. stage hypnotist). For example, there is the belief that you might lose control while in hypnosis. However, it is not possible to hypnotize anyone against their will. With hypnotherapy, you are totally aware of what is being said and at no time do you lose control. Therefore, clinical- or therapeutic hypnosis, assisted by a qualified health professional should never be confused with stage hypnosis. Find out more about the most common myths related to hypnosis.
